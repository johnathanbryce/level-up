## (Reference) Standard structure + tips

This is the round most important to nail because **it IS the job in miniature** — find a business problem, design an AI solution, and reason about value. Good news: it's the single most *coachable* round, and now confirmed to reward thinking over performance.

## What it almost certainly is

One of two variants (ask which):

- **Variant A — "Pick/assigned a business use case and present a solution."** They give you (or you choose) a business scenario — e.g. "AI to reduce Customer Support handle time" or "GenAI for the Sales team's proposal drafting" — and you present how you'd identify, prototype, deploy, and scale an AI solution for it, including ROI/KPIs. **Most likely for this role**, because it mirrors the day-to-day.
- **Variant B — "Walk us through a past AI project as a customer-facing pitch."** You present a real thing you built (Caseway) as if selling it to a business audience. Less likely as the *sole* format, but your Caseway story should be presentation-ready regardless.

Either way, **the audience is mixed** — technical people AND business people in the room. You must prove you understand the tech *and* make the business value land. That dual-audience balance is the #1 thing they score.

## The structure that wins (steal this skeleton)

1. **Agenda / table of contents** (10 sec) — orients the room.
2. **Executive summary** (30 sec) — the problem, the solution, the impact, in plain language. They should know where you're going before you get there.
3. **The business problem — ZERO mention of AI.** Make them *feel the pain* of the current state (the manual work, the cost, the slow turnaround, the risk). This is the most-cited pro move: if you open with AI, you get polite nods; if you open with pain, you create urgency.
4. **The solution** — NOW introduce the AI. Tie each capability back to a specific pain from step 3.
5. **How it works (the architecture)** — a simple diagram. Right level of technical depth: enough that the engineers trust you, simple enough that the business people follow. RAG pipeline, data sources, model, guardrails.
6. **Responsible AI / risk** — privacy, security, bias, data protection. **Non-negotiable at Diligent** (GRC company). This is a differentiator most candidates underweight — you won't.
7. **ROI / KPIs / metrics** — how you measure success. Time saved, cost reduced, % deflected, accuracy. Put a number on it even if estimated, and defend it.
8. **Rollout / scale plan** — prototype → pilot → scale. Shows you think past the demo (the JD's "deploy and scale" language).
9. **Q&A / objection handling** — they WILL push back and may role-play a skeptical stakeholder. Calm, curious responses score as high as the slides.

## The biggest mistakes to avoid

- **Too technical.** "Being technical is only 50% of selling." Half the room is business — connect every technical point to a business outcome (cost, risk, speed, competitive advantage).
- **Leading with AI instead of the problem.** Pain first, always.
- **No numbers.** Executives think in cost/risk/speed/ROI. No metric = no urgency.
- **Forgetting responsible AI.** At a GRC company this is table stakes, not a bonus.
- **Reading slides.** Slides support you; they're not the script.

## Your public-speaking plan (the part you actually asked about)

You said you can beat the fear with reps. Here's the rep plan — this is a *training problem*, not a talent problem:

1. **Build the deck early.** The earlier the artifact exists, the more reps you get. Don't wait for the "details."
2. **Practice OUT LOUD, minimum 10 times.** Silent review doesn't build fluency; speaking does. The pros literally say "practice your speech at least 10 times and record yourself."
3. **Record yourself** (phone is fine). Watch it back once per few reps — you'll catch filler words, pace, and where you lose the thread. Brutal but it works fast.
4. **Rehearse the Q&A separately.** Write the 8 hardest questions a skeptic would ask and answer them out loud. Objection-handling is half the score.
5. **Do a live mock with me.** I'll be the mixed panel and push back as a skeptical business stakeholder. We can run this as many times as you want before the round.
6. **Anchor on familiarity.** The fear shrinks when you've said the words 15 times. By rep 15 your nervous system treats it as routine. That's the whole trick — reps convert "performance" into "recall."
