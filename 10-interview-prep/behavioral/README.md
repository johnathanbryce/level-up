# Behavioral Interview Questions

Curated behavioral questions for mid-to-senior engineering interviews. Use the **STAR format** (Situation, Task, Action, Result) for answers. The goal is to have 5-8 well-rehearsed stories that you can flex to fit different question framings.

These are practiced **verbally**, not written. Reading a STAR answer is useless — the muscle is delivery.

---

## How to use this list

1. **Identify your stories first.** Before drilling questions, list 5-8 real situations from your work history that demonstrate different qualities: technical problem-solving, conflict resolution, learning from failure, leading without authority, owning a project end-to-end, dealing with ambiguity, giving/receiving feedback. Most behavioral questions can be answered by flexing one of these stories.

2. **Map stories to question types.** Each question below has a "what they're really asking" note. Match the story that best fits.

3. **Practice out loud.** Time yourself. STAR answers should be 2-4 minutes — long enough to be substantive, short enough to not bore the interviewer.

4. **End every answer with what you learned or what you'd do differently.** This signals self-awareness and growth.

---

## Tier 1 — The Inevitable Questions

### 1. "Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a coworker."
- **What they're asking:** Can you handle interpersonal friction without burning bridges?
- **Story qualities:** Disagreement was substantive (not personal), you sought to understand the other side, you reached a constructive outcome (even if not your original position).
- **Avoid:** Making the other person look bad, framing yourself as the hero, picking a story where you "won."

### 2. "Tell me about a project you're most proud of."
- **What they're asking:** What do you actually care about? What does "good work" mean to you?
- **Story qualities:** Clear ownership, measurable impact, technical depth or scope, what made it hard.
- **Avoid:** Picking something where you were a small contributor. They want to see *you* in the story.

### 3. "Tell me about a time you failed."
- **What they're asking:** Are you self-aware? Can you learn from mistakes without being defensive?
- **Story qualities:** Real failure (not "my biggest weakness is I work too hard"), clear reflection, concrete change you made afterward.
- **Avoid:** Failures that weren't your fault. Failures that are too small to matter. Failures you blame on others.

### 4. "Why are you leaving your current job?"
- **What they're asking:** Are you running away from something or running toward something?
- **Story qualities:** Forward-looking ("I want to grow into X / work on Y / learn Z"), specific to the new role.
- **Avoid:** Bashing your current employer. Vague "I want a change" answers. Money as the primary reason.

### 5. "Tell me about a time you had to make a decision with incomplete information."
- **What they're asking:** Can you make calls under ambiguity without freezing or recklessly guessing?
- **Story qualities:** Real ambiguity, the process you used (gather what you could, identify reversibility, consult who you needed to), the outcome, what you learned.

---

## Tier 2 — Leadership & Collaboration

### 6. "Tell me about a time you led without formal authority."
- Influencing peers, championing a technical change, mentoring a junior, driving a cross-team initiative.

### 7. "Tell me about a time you gave difficult feedback."
- Code review with a senior engineer, calling out a flawed approach, raising a concern with a manager.

### 8. "Tell me about a time you received difficult feedback."
- How you reacted, what you did with it, whether your behavior actually changed.

### 9. "Tell me about a time you disagreed with a technical decision."
- Did you commit and disagree, or did you fight it? When did you choose which? What was the outcome?

### 10. "Tell me about a time you had to push back on a stakeholder."
- Product wanted X by Friday, you knew it would create technical debt or break something. How did you handle it?

---

## Tier 3 — Technical Judgment

### 11. "Tell me about a time you simplified something complex."
- Refactor, deletion of dead code, replacing a custom solution with a library, architectural simplification.

### 12. "Tell me about a time you debugged a really hard problem."
- The harder and weirder the better. They want to see your diagnostic process.

### 13. "Tell me about a time you had to learn something quickly."
- New language, new framework, new domain. Specifically: what was your strategy for getting up to speed?

### 14. "Tell me about a time you advocated for engineering quality."
- Test coverage, code review standards, observability, refactoring time.

### 15. "Tell me about a time you balanced shipping fast with doing it right."
- Real trade-off. What did you ship, what did you defer, what did you learn from the choice?

---

## Tier 4 — Curveballs

### 16. "Tell me about a time you changed your mind about something significant."
- Technical opinion, career direction, working style. Shows intellectual humility.

### 17. "Tell me about a time you were wrong publicly."
- Code review, design review, all-hands. How did you handle it?

### 18. "What's something you've worked on recently that you're not sure was the right approach?"
- Variant of the failure question, but harder because it's recent and ongoing. Tests current self-awareness.

---

## Notes on usage

- **Build your story list first.** Questions are infinite; stories are finite. Map stories to questions, not the other way around.
- **Practice with Claude playing interviewer.** Ask Claude to drill you on 3-5 questions in a row, give honest critiques on delivery, and push for specifics when answers are vague.
- **Time-box answers at 2-4 min.** Use a timer. Long-winded answers signal poor communication.
- **Always close with what you learned.** Even "good outcome" stories should end with reflection, not just bragging.
